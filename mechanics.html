11  Classical Mechanics

Classical mechanics is the study of motion and forces on macroscopic objects. It forms the foundation of physics and provides the framework for understanding how objects move and interact in our everyday world.

11.1 Introduction to Mechanics

Mechanics deals with the relationship between force, matter, and motion. It is divided into three main areas:

  1. Kinematics - The description of motion without considering forces
  2. Dynamics - The study of forces and their effect on motion
  3. Statics - The study of objects at rest or in equilibrium

11.2 Kinematics

11.2.1 Position and Displacement

The position of an object is described by its coordinates in space. For motion in one dimension, we use the position function \(x(t)\), where \(t\) is time.

Displacement is the change in position: \[\Delta x = x_2 - x_1\]

11.2.2 Velocity and Speed

Average velocity is defined as: \[v_{avg} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1}\]

Instantaneous velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time: \[v = \frac{dx}{dt}\]

11.2.3 Acceleration

Average acceleration: \[a_{avg} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_2 - v_1}{t_2 - t_1}\]

Instantaneous acceleration: \[a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}\]

11.3 Equations of Motion

For constant acceleration, we have the following equations of motion:

  1. \[v = v_0 + at\]
  2. \[x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\]
  3. \[v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\]
  4. \[x - x_0 = \frac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)t\]

where: - \(v_0\) is the initial velocity - \(x_0\) is the initial position - \(a\) is the constant acceleration

11.4 Dynamics

11.4.1 Newton’s Laws of Motion

First Law (Law of Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an external force.

Second Law: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass: \[F = ma\]

Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

11.4.2 Forces

Common types of forces include:

  • Gravitational force: \(F_g = mg\) (near Earth’s surface)
  • Normal force: The perpendicular contact force between surfaces
  • Friction force: \(F_f = \mu N\) (where \(\mu\) is the coefficient of friction)
  • Tension force: Force transmitted through strings or cables

11.5 Energy and Work

11.5.1 Work

Work is defined as the force applied over a distance: \[W = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{s}\]

For constant force: \[W = Fd\cos\theta\]

where \(\theta\) is the angle between force and displacement.

11.5.2 Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion: \[KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\]

11.5.3 Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy: \[PE_g = mgh\]

Elastic potential energy (spring): \[PE_{spring} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2\]

11.5.4 Conservation of Energy

The total mechanical energy is conserved in the absence of non-conservative forces: \[E_{total} = KE + PE = \text{constant}\]

11.6 Momentum

11.6.1 Linear Momentum

Linear momentum is defined as: \[\vec{p} = m\vec{v}\]

11.6.2 Conservation of Momentum

In the absence of external forces, the total momentum of a system is conserved: \[\sum \vec{p}_{initial} = \sum \vec{p}_{final}\]

11.6.3 Collisions

  • Elastic collisions: Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved
  • Inelastic collisions: Only momentum is conserved

11.7 Rotational Motion

11.7.1 Angular Quantities

  • Angular displacement: \(\theta\)
  • Angular velocity: \(\omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt}\)
  • Angular acceleration: \(\alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt}\)

11.7.2 Rotational Dynamics

Torque: \(\tau = rF\sin\theta\)

Moment of inertia: \(I = \sum mr^2\)

Rotational analog of Newton’s second law: \(\tau = I\alpha\)

11.7.3 Angular Momentum

Angular momentum: \(\vec{L} = I\vec{\omega}\)

Conservation of angular momentum: In the absence of external torques, angular momentum is conserved.

11.8 Simple Harmonic Motion

11.8.1 Spring-Mass System

For a mass-spring system, the equation of motion is: \[m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -kx\]

The solution is: \[x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)\]

where: - \(\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}\) is the angular frequency - \(A\) is the amplitude - \(\phi\) is the phase constant

11.8.2 Pendulum

For a simple pendulum (small angles): \[T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\]

where \(T\) is the period and \(L\) is the length of the pendulum.

11.9 Applications

Classical mechanics has numerous applications in:

  • Engineering: Design of structures, machines, and vehicles
  • Astronomy: Planetary motion and orbital mechanics
  • Sports: Analysis of athletic performance
  • Transportation: Vehicle dynamics and safety

Example: The trajectory of a projectile can be analyzed using kinematic equations, making it possible to predict where it will land.

11.10 Summary

Classical mechanics provides the fundamental framework for understanding motion and forces. The concepts introduced here form the basis for more advanced topics in physics and are essential for many practical applications.

11.11 Key Equations

  • Newton’s Second Law: \(F = ma\)
  • Kinematic equations for constant acceleration
  • Work-Energy theorem: \(W = \Delta KE\)
  • Conservation of momentum: \(\sum \vec{p} = \text{constant}\)
  • Simple harmonic motion: \(x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)\)
